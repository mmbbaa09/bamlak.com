ToyotaSupra.

The Toyota Supra has been part of the automotive lexicon for over 40 years . Its roots trace back to the original 1979 Toyota Celica Supra, the middle name a reference to its shared chassis with Toyotaâ€™s smaller Celica sports coupe. The two models eventually split in the 1980s, with the six-cylinder Supra sports coupe dropping the Celica name altogether. After evolving through four generations, Toyota's mighty Supra sales declined in the late 1990s and Toyota pulled the car from the U.S. market in 1998. (Sales continued in Japan into the early 21st century.) Despite failing to show their love with their wallets, the Supra established itself as a legend, paving the way two decades later for a redux. Toyota finally filled the Supra-sized hole in our hearts with a new model in 2020. Since then it's won our 10Best award and has even found a spot in our fleet of long-term test cars.
